Second-Harmonic Dispersion-Interferometer (SHDI): high-precision density measurements.

Unlike conventional two-arm, density-measuring interferometers, the SHDI is more stable, less complex, and lower cost. A simplified depiction of the SHDI is shown in the Figure, which uses a single laser beam source to provide a common-mode beam path and homodyne detection. The ultimate lower limit of sensitivity depends on the sample's index of refraction and thickness, and essentially any type of dispersive medium can be measured, including: plasma, neutral gas, liquids, solids. To date, SHDI's have been used primarily for the measurement of neutral gas density and the electron density in plasma, however, essentially any type of transparent dispersive medium can be sampled, including liquids and solids.

The SHDI instrument is currently available in two configurations for: 1-Dimensional, line-of-sight measurements and for 2-Dimensional, transverse-imaging measurements. The primary characteristics of the SHDI are as follows:

  •   • Vibration isolation systems are typically unnecessary.
  •   • The two compact optical units may be mounted directly on the experiment.
  •   • The instrument can measure large, and small sample sizes, from mm's - m's .
  •   • The system is designed for the application, in order to keep cost low.
  •   • The time resolution can range from Δt ~ ns - seconds.
  •   • The phase resolution can range from, ΔΦ ~ 10-2 - radians.
  •   • Bandwidth and sensitivity can be tailored for measurement requirements.
  •   • As a 2-D instrument, the SHDI can sample mm2 – cm2 areas.
  •   • The recording rate can approach 1,000 fps.
  •   • Large gradients, beam deflections, and fringe jumps may limit the spatial resolution.
  •   • User-friendly software provides for quick-image visualization and data analysis.
